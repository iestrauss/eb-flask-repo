Find the motive

Let's look at another.

So can yoga cure hair loss? The article seems to think it does.

Take a moment and find the publication's motive. Look closer at the publication: The Art of Living. As you click around the site, you'll quickly notice that The Art of Living is a company that sells yoga classes at $195 a pop.

You see where this is going: They may not be the best source for accurate information about the health benefits of yoga. Of course, everyone is biased, but when the bias is just staring you in the face like that ...

It may not always be this easy to figure out a publication's motives. You might run into a legitimate-looking news publication publishing articles like "80% of Americans should be on antidepressants." When you check out their "About Us" page, you'll find they're funded by a foundation. And when you Google the foundation, you'll find it's run by a pharmaceutical company selling antidepressants.

A lot of "shocking" stories about politicians come from fake publications like this. A good rule of thumb: if an article claims a public figure is a serial killer or a kidnapper, and police apparently don't care ... Don't just believe it. Investigate.
